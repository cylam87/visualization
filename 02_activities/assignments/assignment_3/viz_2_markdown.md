The dataset is https://data.ontario.ca/dataset/bumble-bee-diversity-and-abundance-survey 

1. What software did you use to create your data visualization?
Excel

2. Who is your intended audience? 
Scientists, government officials and members of the general public who are 
interested in year-to-year trends in bee abundance across Ontario. 

3. What information or message are you trying to convey with your visualization? 
The abundance of one particular species of bees collected in 4 consecutive years
and in agricultural or semi-natural settings. Males are far more abundant in 
agricultural sites than semi-natural sites, which means bee colonies in 
agricultural sites are healthier (number of males is an indication of colony
health).

4. What aspects of design did you consider when making your visualization? How 
did you apply them? With what elements of your plots? 
The image is 2D with a clean layout and geometric shapes & lines to abide by the 
4 conventions of data visualization. 
I limited extraneous cogniive load by using a familiar chart type (bar), 
absolute values instead of relational, and concise composition (few elements). 
I utilized Gestalt principles by using similarity in color to differentiate the
3 categories of bees. 
I used a combo of two categorical variables in the x axis, and demarcated one of 
them (site type) using axes ticks to clearly separate the 2 levels.

5. How did you ensure that your data visualizations are reproducible? If the 
tool you used to make your data visualization is not reproducible, how will this 
impact your data visualization? 
I used excel. I tried to maximize reproducibility by including both raw and 
processed data in the excel sheet, so anyone can reproduce the underlying plot
using the processed data. However visual elements in the plot (title, labels, 
colors) are not reproducible, so trying to replicate this plot may need to 
slightly different visualizations that may affect its message/accessibility/etc.
    
6. How did you ensure that your data visualization is accessible?  
I used accessible colors from an accessible color palette generator. They are 
high-contrast yet not overly bright. 
The chart title, axes labels, legend and caption include all relevant info. 
I used the accessible typeface Helvetica on all text and avoided switching 
between fonts unnecessarily. 

7. Who are the individuals and communities who might be impacted by your 
visualization?  
Those who care about bee abundances and distributions in Ontario. For example, 
bee farmers and government officials from the Ministry of Agriculture could 
glean useful information on trends in bee abundance across the province (e.g. 
the trend in number of males in each site type). This may drive decisions and 
policies, e.g. improve availability of bee resources in natural sites to improve
colony health there. 

8. How did you choose which features of your chosen dataset to include or exclude 
from your visualization? 
To simplify the plot I aggregated # of bees across sites for each county and 
only presented data on 1 of 14 bee species. I did so as plotting all sites 
separately and all 14 species would overwhelm the viewer with too much 
information and would prevent them from identifying trends in abundance over 
time. 

9. What ‘underwater labour’ contributed to your final data visualization product?
Government staff collected bee samples in the field. 
I used an accessible color palette from an online source.
